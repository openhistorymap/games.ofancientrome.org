# Design context — games.ofancientrome.org

**The concept: the travertine gaming table.** Where `rulers.ofancientrome.org` is
an inscription cut in stone (lapidary, epigraphic, solemn), this is the *same
stone at play* — the scratched gaming boards Romans cut into the steps of the
Basilica Julia, the tavern tables, the temple pavements. The page is a worn
travertine surface; the games sit on it like boards waiting for counters. Warmer
and more playful than its rulers sibling, but unmistakably of the same hand: gold
hairlines, Roman capitals, square edges, pigment-as-meaning.

## Palette

- **Ground:** travertine — warm bone/parchment (`--stone` `#efe6d2` light;
  `#16110b` deep-stone dark). Raised panels and sunk "niches" are one and two
  steps darker. The board thumbnails sit in a recessed niche, as if cut into the
  surface.
- **Accents:** **terracotta** `#a8412e` (the primary — fired clay, the colour of
  a counter and of the play button) and **bronze/gold** `#b88a3e` (hairlines,
  the "reconstruction" tag, focus rings).
- **Category pigments** carry meaning and are never mere fills — a dot, a rotated
  square, a chip edge, the active filter:
  - war & hunt → **olive** `#7a8a3a`
  - race & tables → **terracotta** `#b4472e`
  - alignment → **tessera blue** `#3a6ea5`
  - dice & lots → **gold** `#caa05a`
  - hand & guess → **amethyst** `#8a5a8a`
- **Counters** are terracotta (`ruber`) and bone (`album`); war games use bone vs
  slate. These same two inks read across boards, pieces legend and pills.

## Type

- **Display:** Cinzel (Roman inscriptional capitals) — the masthead, game names,
  section headers. Uppercase, letter-spaced, like a chiselled label.
- **Body:** Spectral — a humanist serif with enough warmth for long rules and
  history without feeling like a manuscript.
- Fallbacks: Marcellus SC / Cardo / Georgia, to match the rulers siblings if the
  web fonts fail.

## The boards

Every board is a generated inline **SVG** — incised lines on a travertine field,
a double frame, marked points as sunk studs. This is deliberate: it ties the
visual language to the *real* Roman boards (which survive precisely as incised
lines on stone), and it keeps the repo asset-free. The wheel (`rota`), the
triangle (`delta`) and the morris lattice (`terni-lapilli`) are drawn as the
graffiti diagrams they are; the race boards (`tabula`, `duodecim-scripta`) get
points and a central rosette; the dice and guessing games get a bare table with a
ghosted word (`ALEA`, `VENVS`, `PAR · IMPAR`) the way a real lusoria board was
captioned.

## Motion & restraint

- Cards lift a few pixels on hover and take on their category pigment at the
  border. The detail overlay rises and fades once. Nothing loops, nothing
  pulses — the stillness of stone, broken only when *you* pick up a piece (which
  happens in the launcher, not here).
- The site itself never animates a board. It is a shelf; the *table* is the
  launcher.

## Voice

Plain, a little wry, scholarly but not stuffy — the register of someone who knows
the Forum graffiti and wants you to play. Where rules are lost, the voice is
explicit and honest about it (the reconstruction banner is not hidden in small
print). The footer is the OHM house line, always: *Made with <3 in Bologna by
OpenHistoryMap.*

## What "good" looks like here

A visitor lands, immediately sees the boards as boards (not icons), reads one
tagline, clicks, understands in ten seconds *what the game is, when it was played,
and how* — and then opens a real table with a friend in one more click. The
history grounds the play; the play earns the history.
